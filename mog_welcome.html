Welcome to MOG, the Mapping Overlaps Gadget for Local Authorities in England and Wales, and Health and Social Care Trusts in Northern Ireland, and Health and Social Care Boards in Scotland.


MOG is a Shiny app for visualising neighbourhood (LSOA) level data in England and Wales by Local Authority. You can also visualise SOA level data for Northern Ireland by Health and Social Care Trust, and Data Zone level data in Scotland by Health and Social Care Board It combines a collection of open data from the Office of National Statistics (ONS), the Consumer Data Research Centre (CDRC), the Department for Communities and Local Government (DCLG), and the Department for Transport (DfT), StatsWales, NISRA (Northern Ireland), and Statistics.Gov.Scot and National Records of Scotland (Scotland).

All data is at the Lower Super Output Area level (or equivalent in NI or Scotland). There are 104 variables for England, 82 variables for Wales, and 45 shared variables between England and Wales. There are 50 variables for Northern Ireland and 49 for Scotland, a full list of which can be found below. At the moment, the data included comes from the following government open data sources:

  • Data from the Indices of Multiple Deprivation 2019
  • Data from the 2011 Census relating to Ethnicity
  • Life Expectancy at Birth (2009-2013)
  • Median House Price (2017 & 2018)
  • Journey Time Statistics for Key Services (2017)
  • Access to Healthy Assets and Hazards (AHAH) 2017
  • StatsWales
  • Northern Ireland Statistics and Research Agency
  • Northern Ireland Indices of Multiple Deprivation 2017
  • Scottish Indices of Multiple Deprivation & Indicators 2020
  • Scottish Census Data (2011)

The MOG contains two features:

  • Univariate Maps
  • Overlap (Bivariate) Maps

Univariate maps are the kind of maps that are available on many websites, for example, from the CDRC.

The MOG also allows you to create simple bivariate, or overlap, maps. These types of maps combine values of two different variables to assess their relationship by mixing their colour palette. The idea and templates for some of the code came from this blog post by Timo Grossenbacher, who created a far more beautiful static bivariate map.

The MOG education version also allows for simple correlations, ANOVA tests, and linear models.

I hope that people will find some interesting ways to use the MOG. I’ve written a blog post, hosted here, with an example of how the maps can be used for scoping out potential research projects or local policy initiatives.

The MOG was created with social workers and support workers in mind, as a way for people to explore the socioeconomic and structural pressures people they work with might experience. I developed a resources about why I think this is important that you can read by clicking here.

If you have any comments or suggestions you can find my contact details here. If you would like to add data, report issues, or modify the source code in any way, it is available on github.

Best wishes and thanks for trying out the MOG,

Cal

Known bugs:

  • Occassionally a neighbourhood is rendered with a non-transparent fill making it look like the value is more extreme.
  • Univariate maps for house price data (and possibly other data with missing values) do not plot correctly in univariate plots. They do work in bivariate plots. Fixed 13.01.2019
  • Hoverbox info only appears when hovering over boundaries, which can make it unclear which boundary it’s referring to.
